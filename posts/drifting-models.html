Drifting Models

A new generative-modeling paradigm. Instead of training a network to predict a noise-to-data trajectory and then walking it iteratively at inference, Drifting Models iterate at training time: each gradient step nudges the network's output distribution toward the data, until the two match. The result is a one-step generator that hits FID 1.54 on ImageNet 256² in latent space and 1.61 in pixel space — state of the art among single-step methods, and competitive with 500-NFE diffusion.

01 What

What is a Drifting Model?

A single-pass neural generator that maps noise to data in one forward pass. The defining move isn't the architecture — it's where the iteration lives. Diffusion and flow matching iterate at inference; Drifting Models iterate at training.

Same problem, different axis

Generative modeling = find f such that the pushforward f#pprior ≈ pdata. Diffusion factors f into many small per-step transformations applied at inference. A Drifting Model keeps f single-pass and lets the training iterations themselves evolve the pushforward distribution.

A field that becomes zero at equilibrium

A "drifting field" Vp,q(x) tells each generated sample which direction to move. It is built to be anti-symmetric — Vp,q = −Vq,p — which forces V = 0 whenever q = p. That zero is the training fixed-point.

One-NFE generation at SOTA quality

On ImageNet 256² (latent space, SD-VAE), a Drifting Model-L/2 hits FID 1.54 in one network evaluation. Best previous one-step result: iMeanFlow-XL/2 at 1.72. Multi-step DiT-XL/2 at 500 NFE: 2.27.

02 How

How does it work?

Three ideas, in order: training-time pushforward, drifting field with an anti-symmetry constraint, and a stop-gradient regression loss that quietly minimises the square of that field. Then a couple of practical levers — feature-space drifting and CFG — that make it work on real images.

2.1 · Training-time pushforward

Pick a generator fθ: ℝC → ℝD. Feed it noise ε ∼ pε. Its output distribution is q = f#pε. SGD turns θ into a sequence θ1, θ2, …, and therefore q into a sequence q1, q2, …. Each parameter update implicitly drifts every generated sample by

Implicit drift induced by a parameter updateΔxi = fi+1(ε) − fi(ε)

The training procedure is the iterative pushforward. Drifting Models design that procedure so its fixed point is q = pdata.

2.2 · The drifting field, and why it must be anti-symmetric

Introduce an explicit field Vp,q(x): ℝd → ℝd that tells each sample how to move:

Desired update rule on samplesxi+1 = xi + Vp, qi(xi)

The field depends on the data distribution p and the current pushforward q. We want V = 0 when q = p — that's equilibrium. The paper enforces this via anti-symmetry:

Proposition 3.1 · anti-symmetry implies zero-drift at equilibriumVp,q(x) = −Vq,p(x) ⇒ q = p ⇒ Vp,q(x) = 0

Two-line proof: when q = p, the subscripts collapse and Vp,p = −Vp,p, so Vp,p = 0. The converse — that V ≈ 0 implies q ≈ p — is not automatic, but the paper gives sufficient conditions for the specific kernelized construction below (Appendix C.1).

The ablation in Table 1 is brutal: every variant of V that breaks anti-symmetry fails catastrophically. The property is doing real work.

2.3 · A concrete V: kernelized attraction + repulsion

Drifting Models instantiate V as a mean-shift-style combination of an attractive field toward data samples and a repulsive field away from generated samples:

Attraction toward positives · repulsion from negativesV+p(x) = 1/Zp · 𝔼y⁺ ∼ p [ k(x, y⁺) · (y⁺ − x) ] V−q(x) = 1/Zq · 𝔼y⁻ ∼ q [ k(x, y⁻) · (y⁻ − x) ] Vp,q(x) := V+p(x) − V−q(x)

Reading that algebraically: the sample gets pulled toward the weighted mean of nearby data points and pushed away from the weighted mean of nearby generated peers. k is a similarity kernel — they pick k(x, y) = exp(−‖x − y‖ / τ), normalised with a softmax over y (so it looks just like InfoNCE in contrastive learning). The form is manifestly anti-symmetric.

2.4 · The training loss · a stop-gradient regression that minimises ‖V‖²

At equilibrium, the generator should satisfy the fixed-point f(ε) = f(ε) + V(f(ε)). Turn that into a loss by stopping the gradient on the target:

Eq. 6 · drift-as-target regression with frozen targetℒ = 𝔼ε [ ‖ fθ(ε) − stopgrad( fθ(ε) + Vp, qθ(fθ(ε)) ) ‖² ]

That loss numerically equals 𝔼[‖V(f(ε))‖²], but you don't have to back-propagate through V (which depends on the entire distribution q). Instead, you regress the network's current output toward its drifted version, treating that target as a frozen tutor. The same trick appears in SimSiam and consistency-model training, but here it's load-bearing for a brand-new training paradigm.

2.5 · The whole training step in 6 lines

Algorithm 1 from the paper, with the simplest reading: in a batch, the same generated samples serve as the negatives, and a parallel batch of real data provides positives.

# Inputs:
#   f_theta     - the generator (DiT-style single-pass network)
#   y_pos       - batch of real data samples drawn from p_data

e        = randn([N, C])                       # noise batch
x        = f_theta(e)                          # pushforward to q
y_neg    = x                                   # reuse as negatives (free)

V        = compute_V(x, y_pos, y_neg)          # Eq. 11, kernel + softmax
x_target = stopgrad(x + V)                     # frozen drifted target

loss     = mse_loss(x, x_target)               # Eq. 6 — drift-as-target regression

That's it. There is no SDE, no ODE solver, no diffusion-time variable, no consistency-along-trajectory constraint, no discriminator network. The only ingredients are a kernel, an anti-symmetric drift, and a stop-gradient regression.

2.6 · Two practical levers

Drifting in feature space (not pixels)

The kernel needs to measure semantic similarity. Doing it in pixel space is too noisy. The paper computes the drifting loss in the feature space of a pretrained self-supervised encoder (MoCo, SimCLR, or a custom latent-MAE that operates directly on the SD-VAE latents — best performer). Multi-scale ResNet features make the gradients richer. Pretrained features are training-time only — no extra inference cost. Without a feature encoder, the method does not work on ImageNet — kernels in raw pixel/latent space are too "flat" to provide signal. This is the most important practical caveat.

Training-time CFG that preserves 1-NFE

Classifier-Free Guidance is reformulated as a mixture in the negative-sample distribution: take some negatives from generated samples (the usual q) and some from unconditional real data. Solving the equilibrium yields qθ(·|c) ≈ α pdata(·|c) − (α−1) pdata(·|∅) — exactly the CFG formula, but realised through a training-time data choice. The CFG scale α is conditioned into the network like a class token and varied freely at inference. The 1-NFE property survives.

2.7 · Inference is one forward pass

No iterative solver. No noise schedule. No second pass for CFG. The generator does what a generator was supposed to do.

03 Why

Why does it matter?

A clean conceptual contribution that also happens to win the benchmark. Three reasons to care.

3.1 · It moves the iteration to the right place

The core conceptual claim

For a decade, the cheapest available approximation to "match the data distribution" was: define a complex map, factor it into easy local steps, and walk those steps at inference. Drifting Models point out that the cheapest local steps you can actually take are the SGD steps you're already taking anyway. There is no second source of iteration needed. The training trajectory is the pushforward path. Once that's said out loud, several pieces fall away: no ODE/SDE formulation, no noise schedule, no consistency loss, no teacher, no discriminator.

3.3 · The lessons buried in the ablations

Anti-symmetry is everything

Table 1: every destructive variant that breaks the Vp,q = −Vq,p structure fails catastrophically. It's not a regulariser — it's the architecture of the equilibrium.

More positives and more negatives both help

Table 2: under fixed compute, raising Npos from 1 to 64 cuts FID from 20.4 → 8.5. Raising Nneg alongside helps further. Same intuition as contrastive learning — larger sample sets make the field estimate sharper.

Feature encoder quality dominates

Table 3: MoCo and SimCLR work. A custom latent-MAE works better. Wider latent-MAE + longer pre-training + classifier-finetuned latent-MAE keep dropping FID. Without any feature encoder, the method doesn't converge on ImageNet. This is the most important practical caveat — pick the encoder carefully.

Robustness to mode collapse · a bonus property

The 2D toy experiments show that even when q is initialised collapsed on a single mode of a bimodal p, training recovers both modes. Intuition: a collapsed q is far from un-attracted data modes, so attraction toward those modes dominates and pulls samples out. This is structurally different from GANs, where mode collapse is the classical failure mode and a constant source of pain.

3.4 · Honest limits

What it nails

  • One-NFE at SOTA quality — no distillation from a teacher, no adversarial training.
  • Parameter-efficient — B/2 (133M) matches XL/2 baselines (~675M).
  • No SDE/ODE machinery — conceptually decoupled from diffusion entirely.
  • Robust to mode collapse by construction of the attraction–repulsion field.
  • CFG slots in naturally as a training-time mixing of the negative distribution, with the 1-NFE property preserved.
  • Plain SGD — no min-max optimisation, no two-network warm-up dance.

Where it bends

  • Requires a strong feature encoder. Without an SSL feature space, kernels are too flat on ImageNet and training does not converge. Choice of k, τ, and φ is load-bearing.
  • Quadratic-in-batch cost. Pairwise kernels over positives × negatives × samples scale with batch. The paper uses batch ≈ 4096 to estimate V well — not a problem for ImageNet research clusters, but worth knowing.
  • No exact-likelihood interpretation. Unlike normalizing flows / VAEs, there's no ELBO or change-of-variable density to report. Loss equals ‖V‖², not log p(x).
  • Empirical convergence story, not provable. Anti-symmetry guarantees q = p ⇒ V = 0; the reverse implication relies on identifiability conditions discussed in Appendix C.1, not a clean theorem.
  • Single domain so far. Demonstrated only on class-conditional ImageNet 256² (latent and pixel). Open: text-to-image, video, audio.

3.5 · Where this could go

Three speculations on what this paper enables:

Real-time generative video. Multi-step diffusion is the bottleneck for live applications. A 1-NFE generator at sub-second per frame, with this quality, removes that bottleneck — provided the feature-encoder requirement can be met for the temporal domain.

An alternative to GANs without the pathology. Drifting Models share the "single-pass generator + non-adversarial loss" property with GANs but optimise something convex-ish (a regression to a frozen target) instead of a saddle-point objective. If the formulation scales, this could be the GAN replacement that GANs themselves never became.

A unifying lens. Many recent few-step methods (Consistency, MeanFlow, AdvFlow) can be re-read through the drifting-field lens: each defines an implicit "target the model is regressing toward" each step. Drifting Models simply make that target explicit and as principled as possible.

The thing I'd want to see next: replace ImageNet with COCO-style text-to-image. The conditioning machinery already supports arbitrary signals via adaLN-zero — CFG slots in cleanly. If the feature encoder generalises to natural-image distributions (CLIP, DINOv2), this paradigm could be a serious one-step competitor to SD3 / FLUX-class diffusion models.
